Hubble Space Telescope

The History

The Birth of Hubble

Built to observe the universe beyond the blur of Earth's atmosphere, Hubble became one of the most important scientific instruments ever launched. Its mission changed both astronomy and the public image of space exploration.

Hubble's first mirror was polished with extraordinary precision, but to the wrong curve by a tiny margin: enough to blur its first images until astronauts corrected the flaw in orbit.

How It Was Made

The telescope combines optics, instruments, communications systems and solar arrays into a compact orbital observatory. Every section has a precise role, from collecting light to sending data back home.

Launch Into Space

Launch Into Space

Hubble launched on April 25, 1990 aboard Space Shuttle Discovery and began its long orbit around Earth, opening a new way of seeing deep space.

The telescope traveled to orbit inside the shuttle cargo bay. Its deployment turned a single launch into one of the most iconic moments in modern spaceflight.

35 Years of Images

Archive

Thirty five years of observations produced an immense visual archive, from glowing nebulae to distant galaxies and detailed studies of our own cosmic neighborhood.

Hubble Today

Still Working

Even today Hubble continues to send observations back to Earth, extending a scientific legacy that has lasted for decades.

Hubble does not circle the planet once a day: it completes an orbit roughly every 95 minutes, so it sees sunrise and sunset many times in a single Earth day.

James Webb

New telescopes continue the path Hubble helped define, but its images and discoveries remain central to modern astronomy.

Unlike Hubble, James Webb orbits the Sun 1.5 million km away. Its gold mirrors and infrared vision allow it to see through cosmic dust to witness the birth of the very first stars.

The Future of Hubble

Legacy

One day Hubble will end its mission, but the data it produced and the way it reshaped our view of the cosmos will continue to influence science far into the future.

Few observatories were ever designed to be repaired in space. Hubble's service missions extended its life so much that it became a bridge between generations of telescopes.
